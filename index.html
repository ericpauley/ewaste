E-Waste disposal

The Problem

A Massive Problem

In 2009 people in the United States disposed of 216 million electronic devices. 84% of those went into landfills.

Environmental Hazards

E-Waste can contain hamful substances, such as mercury, arsenic, cadmium and selenium.

Unsustainable Production

Only 8 percent of mobile devices which are no longer used are recycled. Other devices are either disposed of or stored. This puts unnecessary resource demand on manufacturers of electronics.

Non-reusable Products

Many devices are obsolete by the time owners are done with them. These devices cannot be reused and so the only options are recycling or disposal.

The United States is facing an electronic crisis. Every year hundreds of millions of electronic devices are disposed of in landfills, eliminating rare materials from use and allowing harmful substances to enter our environment. While the technology exists to remanufacture the materials in these devices, many consumers are not aware of this, and so they either keep their old devices or throw them away.

While electronic devices are generally harmless when used, they are often damaged when thrown away which allows the toxic inner substances to leak into the soil, causing damage to the environment.

Solutions

Recycling E-Waste

By recycling E-Waste, the energy and materials that would have gone into making new devices can be saved, reducing the cost of new devices while keeping harmful materials out of landfills. There are thousands of recycling centers across the United States who accept E-Waste, and taking advantage of these services as opposed to throwing out devices has innumerable advantages:

Heavy Metals

By recycling one million cell phones, 36000 pounds of metals can be recovered, including 772 pounds of silver and 75 pounds of gold.

Toxic Materials

By recycling or properly disposing of the harmful materials in electronic devices, these materials are kept out of landfills and the soil surrounding them.

Reduced Waste

Over 100 million pounds of materials are recovered from electronics recycling every year. These resources are kept out of landfills.

Better than Mining

Materials recovered from electronic devices can be used in the production of new devices, reducing the cost of raw material extraction.

Helping Others

By reusing or refurbishing devices, people who could otherwise not afford these devices would have access to them at a lower cost.

A Variety of Options

Devices can be recycled in multiple ways, depending on the usefulness of the device. They can be scrapped down for materials, individual components can be reused, or the entire device can be refurbished and used by someone else. The less the device is broken down, the easier it is to recycle or reuse.

Job Creation

The recycling industry creates jobs for those who work to recycle the products, as well as for those who create new products out of the materials, because the cheaper materials encourage growth in other industries.

One million cell phones can be converted to 36000 pounds of raw materials. Your contribution makes a difference.

What you can do

Recycling tips

How to Recycle your E-Waste

Recycling your E-Waste is free and easy, and you can often even get discounts for doing so. State College has multiple locations for E-Waste recycling, including the CCRRA, Best Buy, and others. Below is a map of the E-Waste recycling centers in State College:

Computers

Computers are accepted at any of the above recycling centers. Be sure to clear or your hard disk before taking it to a recycling center, as the disks may be refurbished and reused, putting your data at risk if they are not properly cleared. Recycling centers may be hesitant to accept older technology, so it is best to recycle computers once you are done with them instead of storing them.

Televisions

The above recycling centers accept both old-style CRT TVs and newer LCD TVs. There is nothing you need to do to prepare your TV for recycling, though if you plan on getting a new TV you should keep your old cables as they may be useful when setting up the new one.

Batteries

Alkaline batteries may be thrown away in your regular trash. Rechargeable batteries may be recycled at the above locations and should not be thrown away as they contain harmful materials. Lead Acid batteries such as car batteries may be recycled wherever you got them installed, and it is recommended to do this to take advantage of rebate offers.

Mobile Devices

Mobile devices may be recycled at any of the above locations. Be sure to clear all data from your device. (Instructions for Android and iOS) Retailers may also accept your device when you purchase a new one and give you rebates off your new phone when you give them an old one. See your wireless carrier for details.
